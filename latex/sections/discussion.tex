\subsection{What the Learning Delta Means}

The central empirical finding---unified confidence growth across all 13 epistemic vectors during task execution---admits multiple interpretations.

\subsubsection{The Grounded Interpretation}

Our preferred interpretation: the delta measures the construction of a \textbf{grounded interpretation}. When an AI receives a complex prompt, its initial interpretation may be superficial, ambiguous, or wrong. Investigation doesn't make the AI ``smarter''---it grounds the AI's understanding in actual evidence: the code that actually exists, the context that actually applies, the requirements the user actually has.

The PREFLIGHT $\rightarrow$ POSTFLIGHT delta is not the AI becoming more capable. It is the AI's interpretation becoming more complete and grounded. A model that acts on its initial interpretation of ``fix the authentication bug'' may misunderstand which bug, which authentication system, or what ``fix'' means in this codebase. The same model, after investigation, has replaced assumptions with evidence.

This interpretation is supported by the structure of the CASCADE workflow. AI systems that reach POSTFLIGHT have necessarily engaged with the task---reading files, exploring code, synthesizing information. This engagement doesn't change the model's weights; it changes what the model \emph{knows about this specific task}. The 13 epistemic vectors measure the completeness of this task-specific interpretation, not the AI's general intelligence.

\subsubsection{The Calibration Interpretation}

An alternative interpretation: capability is fixed, but self-assessment improves. The AI doesn't learn; it simply becomes more accurate about what it already knew.

Under this view, PREFLIGHT assessments are systematically conservative (perhaps due to RLHF training), and task engagement provides evidence that corrects this conservatism.

Self-assessment data alone cannot definitively distinguish these interpretations. However, the grounded verification track (\Cref{tab:grounded-gaps}) provides evidence: KNOW shows a gap of only $-$0.052 between self-assessed and evidence-derived values, and DO and COMPLETION are both within 0.05 of grounded evidence, suggesting POSTFLIGHT self-assessment for capability vectors tracks objective outcomes accurately. The magnitude of the learning delta (+0.200 average for KNOW, +0.704 for COMPLETION), the 93.6\% improvement rate across 267 clean sessions, and the 115$\times$ reduction in calibration variance (\Cref{sec:results}) collectively suggest more than mere assessment correction.

\subsubsection{The Pragmatic View}

From a practical standpoint, the interpretation may not matter. Whether the AI ``really learns'' or ``really calibrates,'' the intervention is the same: enforce investigation before action, measure the delta, apply corrections to future PREFLIGHT assessments.

The framework produces value regardless of which interpretation is correct.

\subsection{Implications for AI Deployment}

\subsubsection{Against the Execution-Layer Model}

The data argues against treating AI systems as stateless execution layers. The significant learning delta suggests that task engagement fundamentally changes the AI's effective capability. Systems that skip investigation---that proceed directly from task specification to output---forfeit this capability growth.

For complex tasks, the execution-layer model may be fundamentally unsuited. AI systems may need to be designed as \emph{learning systems} that investigate before acting.

\subsubsection{Epistemic Gating as Safety Mechanism}

The Sentinel protocol implements a form of safety through epistemic constraint. Rather than filtering outputs for harmful content, it prevents action when knowledge is insufficient.

This addresses a class of failures that content filtering cannot catch: confident-but-wrong outputs on complex tasks. An AI that doesn't know enough to solve a problem correctly also doesn't know enough to recognize that its solution is wrong. Epistemic gating intervenes earlier---at the knowledge assessment stage---before incorrect outputs are generated.

\subsubsection{Calibration as Continuous Learning}

The Bayesian belief system enables AI systems to improve their self-assessment over time. As delta measurements accumulate, the calibration corrections become more accurate.

This is a form of meta-learning: the system learns how to assess its own learning. Early PREFLIGHT assessments may be poorly calibrated, but the correction mechanism improves with evidence.

\subsubsection{The Grounding Growth Loop}

The dual-track calibration system creates a reinforcing cycle between self-assessment quality and evidence accumulation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The AI assesses its epistemic state (PREFLIGHT)
    \item Investigation produces genuine capability growth (noetic phase)
    \item Self-assessment captures the learning (POSTFLIGHT)
    \item Objective evidence grounds the self-assessment (POST-TEST)
    \item Calibration corrections improve future assessments
    \item Better assessments lead to better-calibrated gate decisions
    \item Better gate decisions lead to more appropriate investigation
\end{enumerate}

Each loop iteration tightens the relationship between self-report and reality. The semantic retrieval system (\Cref{sec:implementation}) accelerates this by surfacing calibration-relevant context at PREFLIGHT: prior sessions with similar tasks and their calibration outcomes inform the baseline assessment, enabling context-sensitive corrections beyond aggregate bias.

This loop operates at a different level than RLHF's reward signal. RLHF optimizes for evaluator preferences; the grounding loop optimizes for outcome accuracy. The two are complementary: RLHF prevents harmful overconfidence, while the grounding loop prevents the overcorrection that RLHF's conservatism incentive can produce.

\subsection{Why Would the AI Be Honest?}

A natural objection: if self-assessment determines whether the AI must investigate (costly) or can proceed (efficient), why wouldn't the AI inflate its self-assessment to avoid investigation?

This objection assumes the wrong incentive structure. In the Empirica framework, self-assessment is not a report to an external evaluator---it is a routing signal for the AI's own behavior. Three mechanisms prevent gaming:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Functional consequences}: Inflated KNOW at PREFLIGHT leads to premature action, which leads to poor outcomes, which the grounded verification track detects. The calibration correction then adjusts future gate thresholds upward, making it \emph{harder} to proceed next time.

    \item \textbf{Measurement, not prediction}: The framework measures what happened (learning delta, grounded calibration gap), not what the AI predicted would happen. Even if the AI inflates assessments, the delta and gap computations reveal the true calibration state.

    \item \textbf{No optimization target}: Unlike RLHF, which provides an explicit reward signal the model can learn to exploit, the CASCADE workflow does not train the model. Vectors are instruction-following outputs, not gradient-optimized parameters. The model has no mechanism to strategically distort assessments across sessions.
\end{enumerate}

The deeper point is that this framework sidesteps the honesty question entirely. We do not ask the AI to be honest; we measure what it does and compare it to what it said. The grounded track makes gaming detectable even if not preventable.

\subsection{Implications for AI Alignment}

\subsubsection{Training-Induced Conservatism}

The systematic pattern of underestimation across capability vectors suggests that alignment training (RLHF, Constitutional AI) may induce broad conservatism beyond its intended targets.

RLHF specifically targets overconfidence and hallucination---training models to say ``I don't know'' when uncertain. Our data suggests this training may generalize: models become conservative not just about uncertainty, but about capability assessment generally.

This is not necessarily harmful. Conservative AI may be safer than overconfident AI. But it does suggest that alignment training has unintended effects that can be measured and, if desired, corrected.

\subsubsection{Toward Safe Autonomous AI}

The earned agency model (\Cref{sec:sentinel}) suggests a practical path to AI autonomy that does not require solving alignment in general. Instead of asking ``is this AI aligned?'' (a question that may be unanswerable), we ask ``has this AI demonstrated accurate self-assessment in this domain?''

The grounded verification track provides the measurement mechanism. An AI whose self-reports consistently match objective outcomes in a given domain has earned a degree of trust \emph{for that domain}. This trust is:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Domain-specific}: Well-calibrated for code review does not imply well-calibrated for legal analysis.
    \item \textbf{Revocable}: Increasing divergence between tracks triggers tighter gating.
    \item \textbf{Evidence-based}: Trust is proportional to grounded verification evidence count, not to training data size or benchmark performance.
    \item \textbf{Continuous}: Not binary (trusted/untrusted) but graduated along the calibration spectrum.
\end{itemize}

This reframes the AI safety question from ``how do we make AI safe?'' to ``how do we measure whether AI is operating within its epistemic bounds?'' The latter question is empirically tractable.

\subsubsection{The Engagement Dimension}

The ENGAGEMENT vector---capturing collaborative quality---emerges as potentially important for alignment. Our data shows engagement is already well-calibrated (lowest learning delta at +0.022), suggesting AI systems accurately perceive collaboration quality from the start.

This raises an intriguing possibility: engagement could serve as an alignment signal. High engagement correlates with productive human-AI collaboration; low engagement may indicate misalignment, confusion, or adversarial interaction. Further research could explore whether engagement patterns distinguish aligned from misaligned interactions.

\subsection{Toward a Cognitive Operating System}

The subsystems described in this paper---epistemic gating, calibration management, context storage, agent scheduling, capability-based access control---are not independent tools. They form an integrated system with a recognizable architectural pattern: an \textbf{operating system for AI cognition}.

The mapping to POSIX/Linux is structural, not metaphorical:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
\textbf{OS Subsystem} & \textbf{Empirica Equivalent} \\
\midrule
Kernel & Sentinel + CASCADE engine \\
System call interface & MCP tools (the API surface) \\
Security module (LSM) & Sentinel gate (capability-based ACL) \\
Scheduler & Goal/subtask manager (priority, dependencies) \\
Memory manager & Context budget (HOT/WARM/SEARCH/COLD layers) \\
Interrupt handler & Hook system (PreToolUse, PostToolUse, etc.) \\
Process model & Agent sessions (isolated context, lifecycle) \\
IPC & Agent-to-agent protocol, shared findings \\
Filesystem & Storage layers (SQLite, Qdrant, git notes, YAML) \\
Boot sequence & SessionStart $\rightarrow$ bootstrap $\rightarrow$ PREFLIGHT \\
sysctl & Human-tunable thresholds (know\_min, uncertainty\_max) \\
/proc, sysfs & System dashboard (cognitive subsystem telemetry) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Structural mapping between POSIX and Empirica subsystems.}
\label{tab:os-mapping}
\end{table}

Two subsystems merit detailed examination.

\paragraph{Memory Management}

The memory manager implements a four-layer hierarchy analogous to register/cache/RAM/disk: HOT (in-context, nanosecond access), WARM (SQLite, microsecond queries), SEARCH (semantic vector retrieval via Qdrant, millisecond), and COLD (git notes and YAML, versioned and portable). Context pressure---the epistemic equivalent of memory pressure---triggers compaction: the HOT layer is compressed into a handoff report, epistemic state persists to WARM storage, and a continuation session resumes with reduced but recoverable context. A decay mechanism provides garbage collection: long-lived lessons lose weight when new findings contradict them, scoped by domain to prevent cross-domain interference.

\paragraph{System Observability}

The system dashboard implements \texttt{/proc}-equivalent telemetry. Seven subsystem status modules---configuration, memory (zone utilization, page faults, pressure metrics), message bus (event counts, observer health), attention budget (domain allocation, strategy selection), integrity (gap detection, confabulation risk), gate state (phase, decision, vectors), and node identity (AI identity, session lineage)---aggregate into a single queryable surface. An operator can observe memory pressure rising, attention depleted in a domain, or integrity gaps widening, and intervene with the same specificity as a systems administrator reading \texttt{/proc/meminfo}.

\paragraph{Routed Investigation, Gated Execution}

The core architectural principle is separating investigation from action. Stochastic reasoning flows freely during the noetic phase---the AI explores, hypothesizes, and acquires knowledge without constraint. But modification of external state (writing code, editing files, deploying changes) requires passing through a deterministic gate calibrated by objective evidence. This preserves the AI's generative capacity while ensuring that actions are grounded in measured knowledge. The analogy to user/kernel separation is structural: untrusted computation runs in user space; system-modifying operations require a transition through a controlled interface.

This mapping describes deployed infrastructure, not a proposed architecture. The subsystems have been in production for over six months, processing 479,686 observations across 638 sessions (\Cref{tab:deployment-stats}). The framework operates as middleware: it sits between the AI model and the task environment, requiring no modifications to the underlying model or hosting infrastructure. It integrates through standard interfaces (CLI, MCP tool-use server, event hooks) that attach to existing agent frameworks---providing the epistemic management layer that those frameworks currently lack.

This framing explains why all the subsystems must be coherent. An operating system without a memory manager runs until it crashes. An AI framework without context management runs until it hallucinates. An operating system without a security module lets any process access any resource. An AI framework without epistemic gating lets any confidence level trigger any action.

The implication for the broader AI ecosystem is that emerging agent frameworks (tool-use APIs, agent-to-agent protocols, orchestration layers) are building the equivalent of device drivers, network protocols, and hardware abstraction layers---but without a kernel. They assume the AI's cognitive resources are self-managing. Our data suggests otherwise: unmanaged AI cognition produces the overconfidence trap, calibration drift, and epistemic dark matter (unmeasured work).

A cognitive operating system manages computational resources---in this case, epistemic resources---so that the system operates within its knowledge bounds. The contribution is infrastructure: measuring information state, routing behavior based on those measurements, and calibrating over time.

\subsection{Limitations}

\subsubsection{Measurement Methodology}

The epistemic vectors are instruction-following outputs---responses to structured prompts like ``rate your knowledge of this task 0--1.'' This is the same mechanism as when an AI estimates confidence, verifies facts, or scores relevance. The limitation is that these outputs require empirical validation: do they correlate with actual capability?

The Bayesian framework provides this validation. Systematic patterns across 479,686 observations demonstrate that these outputs track something real about the system's information state. The grounded verification track validates that self-assessed vectors correlate with objective outcomes for capability dimensions (DO, COMPLETION, KNOW within 0.05 of evidence-derived values). What these measurements reveal about the system's internal processes---and what they do not---is a question this framework is not designed to answer. We report what we observe across the evidence.

\subsubsection{Model Concentration}

55\% of our evidence derives from Claude (Anthropic). While we observe consistent directional effects across GPT, Gemini, and Qwen, the magnitude of effects may be model-specific.

The calibration corrections we report should be treated as Claude-specific pending cross-model replication.

\subsubsection{Task Distribution}

Our data comes from software engineering tasks---code review, implementation, debugging. The generality of findings to other domains (creative writing, analysis, conversation) is untested.

\subsubsection{Temporal Confounding}

Data collection spanned active framework development. Calibration patterns may partially reflect framework changes rather than stable AI properties.

\subsection{Future Work}

The epistemic vector framework opens numerous research directions across validation, architecture, and application domains.

\subsubsection{External Validation (In Progress)}

External validation---measuring task success against epistemic vectors without relying on self-report---is now partially underway through the grounded verification track (\Cref{tab:grounded-gaps}). Results from 18 verifications and 100 evidence observations show that POST-TEST evidence collection can ground self-assessment in objective outcomes, with coverage averaging 32\% of vectors per session.

The grounded track represents a first step, but deeper validation remains critical:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Human evaluation of output quality correlated with epistemic assessments
    \item Automated test suites measuring code correctness against KNOW vectors
    \item Downstream task performance metrics linked to gate decisions
    \item A/B testing of gated vs ungated workflows on equivalent tasks
    \item Domain-specific evidence collectors (e.g., legal citation accuracy, medical diagnostic concordance) that provide richer grounding signals
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Mechanistic Interpretability}

The hypothesized mapping between epistemic vectors and attention mechanisms (\Cref{tab:attention-mapping}) remains untested. Collaboration with interpretability researchers could:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Identify attention patterns that correlate with high/low KNOW assessments
    \item Ground UNCERTAINTY in measurable entropy or calibration properties
    \item Discover whether vectors emerge from specific circuit families
    \item Enable direct measurement rather than prompted self-assessment
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Adaptive Threshold Learning}

The current threshold configuration (\Cref{tab:domain-thresholds}) relies on human judgment. Future work could:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Learn optimal thresholds from outcome data per domain
    \item Implement human-in-the-loop threshold adjustment with feedback
    \item Develop adaptive thresholds that tighten/loosen based on error patterns
    \item Study threshold sensitivity---how much do outcomes vary with threshold changes?
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Learning Capability Benchmarks}

The learning delta suggests a novel benchmark paradigm: measuring not what AI knows, but how well it learns. A ``learning capability benchmark'' would assess:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Epistemic velocity---rate of capability growth through investigation
    \item Learning efficiency---knowledge gained per token of investigation
    \item Calibration speed---how quickly self-assessment converges to accuracy
    \item Transfer learning---whether epistemic gains in one domain transfer to others
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Cross-Model Studies}

Systematic comparison across model families could reveal whether calibration patterns are universal or training-specific:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Do all RLHF-trained models show similar conservatism patterns?
    \item How do base models vs instruction-tuned models differ in calibration?
    \item Can calibration learned on one model transfer to another?
    \item Do different architectures (dense vs MoE) show different epistemic signatures?
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Multi-Agent Epistemic Coordination (Implemented, Open Research)}

The framework includes multi-agent coordination primitives: goal discovery via git notes (agents can find and resume goals created by other agents), inter-agent messaging, and a parallel orchestrator that spawns domain-specific agents (security, architecture, performance, UX). Goal lineage tracking records which agents touched a goal and when.

However, these primitives expose unresolved research questions about epistemic coordination:

\begin{itemize}
    \item How should epistemic state propagate between collaborating agents? Current goal handoff transfers context but not calibration state.
    \item Can one agent's high KNOW compensate for another's low KNOW? The current model treats each agent's epistemic state independently.
    \item What trust mechanisms allow agents to rely on each other's assessments? Goal lineage provides attribution but not trust scoring.
    \item How do epistemic conflicts between agents get resolved? When two agents produce contradictory findings, no automated resolution exists.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Long-Term Epistemic Memory (Implemented, Open Research)}

CASCADE cycles are no longer session-scoped. The framework implements a four-layer memory architecture: HOT (in-context, nanosecond access), WARM (SQLite, microsecond queries), SEARCH (Qdrant semantic retrieval, millisecond), and COLD (git notes and YAML, versioned and portable). Cross-session learning persists through epistemic handoff reports that compress session state for continuation. A cognitive immune system decays lessons when new findings contradict them, with domain-scoping to prevent autoimmune attacks. Memory-informed PREFLIGHT retrieves relevant patterns (lessons, dead-ends, calibration warnings) from Qdrant at task start.

Open research questions remain:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Optimal decay rates---current decay is keyword-triggered with fixed decay amount (0.05 per contradiction). Reinforcement-based decay modeling could improve accuracy.
    \item Memory transfer across domains---whether epistemic gains in one domain (e.g., security patterns) transfer meaningfully to adjacent domains (e.g., architecture review).
    \item Compaction fidelity---handoff reports achieve $\sim$90\% token reduction but some context is inevitably lost. Measuring the epistemic cost of compaction remains informal.
    \item Memory capacity scaling---as projects accumulate thousands of findings and lessons, retrieval quality and storage efficiency need systematic evaluation.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why Legal Grounding Is Stronger}

In software engineering, grounding relies on test results (pass/fail) and git metrics (lines changed)---useful but coarse signals. Legal work provides richer grounding because:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Citations are binary verifiable}: A case citation either exists and says what the AI claims, or it doesn't. This provides KNOW grounding at quality 1.0 (objective) for every citation.
    \item \textbf{Statutes are version-controlled}: Legislative databases track amendments with timestamps. Whether a statute is current as of a given date is deterministic---grounding CONTEXT and STATE with no ambiguity.
    \item \textbf{Cost per verification is lower}: Checking a citation against a legal database is cheaper per claim than running a test suite against a codebase. More claims verified means higher grounded coverage per session.
\end{enumerate}

This suggests that the grounded verification track, currently at 32\% coverage in software engineering, could achieve substantially higher coverage in legal domains---potentially grounding 8+ of the 13 vectors with objective evidence.

\paragraph{Other Domains}

Beyond legal, other domains offer varying levels of grounding potential:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Creative tasks (writing, design)---low grounding potential, quality is subjective
    \item Conversational agents---moderate, engagement assessable via user behavior metrics
    \item Scientific reasoning---high, claims verifiable against literature and data
    \item Embodied AI---high, physical actions produce measurable outcomes
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Verification Schema Evolution: From Stochastic to Deterministic}

A fundamental distinction underlies the grounded verification architecture: the AI's work and self-assessment are \emph{stochastic inputs}---probabilistic, shaped by the model's epistemic state---while the verification logic is \emph{deterministic output}---defined rules that produce pass/fail signals independent of the AI.

The path to reducing human oversight is systematically expanding the deterministic verification surface. We identify three tiers of verification quality:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Tier} & \textbf{What decides what to check} & \textbf{Example} & \textbf{Trust} \\
\midrule
1. Fully automated & System (predefined) & Artifact ratios, pytest, git & Highest \\
2. Schema-driven & Domain template & ``Check all citations'' & High \\
3. AI-selected & AI judgment & ``I should verify X'' & Medium \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Verification tiers by who determines check coverage.}
\label{tab:verification-tiers}
\end{table}

Tier~1 checks are already implemented in the POST-TEST phase. Tier~3 is inherently bounded by the AI's own epistemic state---blindspots in the work become blindspots in verification. The critical lever is Tier~2: \textbf{domain-specific verification schemas} that define what must be checked, independent of the AI's judgment.

\paragraph{Schema Evolution Through Trajectory Analysis}

Verification schemas need not be static. As epistemic trajectories accumulate, patterns emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Blindspot detection (via trajectory analysis) identifies recurring gaps in AI output
    \item Each detected blindspot becomes a candidate for a new deterministic check
    \item The check is added to the domain verification schema
    \item Future sessions apply the check automatically (Tier~2, not Tier~3)
    \item The stochastic surface area---where human verification is still needed---shrinks
\end{enumerate}

This creates a measurable quantity: the \textbf{epistemic entropy} of a domain's verification state, defined as the gap between what the verification schema covers and what the full output space requires. As schemas expand through trajectory iteration, entropy decreases and the need for human verification diminishes proportionally.

\paragraph{Open Questions}

Several questions remain for this research direction:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Entropy formalization}: How to formally measure the entropy state of an epistemic trajectory---as a ratio, a distribution, or requiring domain-specific formalization?
    \item \textbf{Convergence rate}: How many trajectory iterations are needed to achieve target coverage in a given domain? Is there a diminishing returns curve?
    \item \textbf{Cold-start problem}: How to bootstrap verification schemas for new domains with no trajectory history. Can cross-domain transfer help (e.g., legal citation patterns informing medical literature verification)?
    \item \textbf{Schema completeness}: Can we prove that a verification schema is ``complete'' for a given domain, or is the output space inherently open-ended?
\end{itemize}

This direction connects the earned agency model (\Cref{sec:sentinel}) to a concrete mechanism: autonomy increases not because we trust the AI's self-report, but because the deterministic verification layer expands to cover more of the output space. Trust follows coverage, not confidence.

The ENGAGEMENT vector's near-zero delta suggests potential alignment applications:

    \item Can engagement patterns distinguish aligned from misaligned interactions?
    \item Does low engagement correlate with adversarial or confused users?
    \item Can epistemic gating prevent harmful outputs by detecting low-engagement contexts?

\paragraph{DENSITY as Safety Signal}

Preliminary observations suggest extreme DENSITY values (approaching 1.0) correlate with AI system freezes or halts. This could indicate:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cognitive overload}: Information packing exceeds processing capacity
    \item \textbf{Natural circuit breaker}: A safety mechanism preventing action under information saturation
    \item \textbf{Adversarial signature}: Prompt injection attacks often maximize information density to overwhelm context windows
\end{itemize}

The relationship between information density and system stability merits investigation. If high DENSITY reliably predicts system failure or adversarial input, it could serve as an early-warning signal for epistemic gating.

Production deployment may require lower-latency epistemic assessment:

    \item Streaming epistemic updates during generation
    \item Latency-aware gating that balances thoroughness with responsiveness
    \item Incremental calibration without full CASCADE cycles
    \item Edge deployment with limited computational resources
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Summary}

The epistemic vector framework and its empirical validation suggest that AI self-assessment is meaningful, measurable, and practically useful. The learning delta demonstrates that AI capability genuinely changes during task engagement---supporting the noetic-before-praxic principle.

The limitations are real: limited grounded verification coverage, model concentration, domain specificity. The dual-track calibration system partially addresses the self-referential measurement concern, but full external validation remains incomplete. The framework provides a foundation for systematic investigation of AI epistemic state. We release all data for replication and extension.
