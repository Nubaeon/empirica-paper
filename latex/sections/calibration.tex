% Section 5: Empirical Results - Calibration Analysis

A critical question for any self-assessment framework is \textbf{calibration accuracy}: do AI-reported epistemic vectors correspond to actual task outcomes? We analyzed calibration using Bayesian belief updating across 479,686 evidence observations.

\subsection{Methodology}

For each vector, we maintain a Bayesian belief about the AI's epistemic state, updated via conjugate normal-normal inference. The prior ($\mu_0$) is set from PREFLIGHT self-assessment; observations accumulate through task execution; the posterior reflects the updated belief at POSTFLIGHT.

The \textbf{learning delta} for each vector is:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_i = \mu_{\text{posterior}} - \mu_{\text{prior}}
\end{equation}

Positive delta indicates the vector value \emph{increased} during task execution (confidence growth). For UNCERTAINTY, which operates on an inverted scale, a \emph{negative} delta (uncertainty decreasing) also indicates confidence growth.

\subsection{Pattern Analysis}

Two patterns emerge from the analysis:

\paragraph{Pattern 1: Learning Produces Confidence Growth}

All vectors show movement toward higher confidence during task execution. AI systems begin tasks with conservative self-assessments and converge toward more accurate (higher) assessments as they accumulate evidence through work.

\paragraph{Pattern 2: Magnitude Varies by Vector}

COMPLETION shows the largest delta (+0.704), suggesting AI systems dramatically underestimate task progress at PREFLIGHT. ENGAGEMENT shows near-zero delta (+0.022), suggesting this vector is already well-calibrated from task onset.

\subsection{Calibration Correction}

The learning delta reveals how self-assessments should be corrected. For PREFLIGHT assessments, adding the expected delta yields a more accurate estimate:

\begin{equation}
V_{\text{corrected}} = V_{\text{PREFLIGHT}} + \Delta_V
\end{equation}

For the Sentinel readiness gate (\Cref{sec:sentinel}), this means:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Raw PREFLIGHT know of 0.55 $\rightarrow$ corrected to $\sim$0.75
    \item Raw PREFLIGHT uncertainty of 0.48 $\rightarrow$ corrected to $\sim$0.27
\end{itemize}

The correction compensates for systematic conservatism at task onset.

\subsection{Grounded Verification: Dual-Track Calibration}

The self-referential calibration track (PREFLIGHT $\rightarrow$ POSTFLIGHT) measures learning trajectory but cannot answer a harder question: \emph{does the AI's post-task self-assessment match what actually happened?} To address this, we introduced a grounded verification track that compares POSTFLIGHT vectors against objective post-test evidence.

\subsubsection{Evidence Collection}

After each POSTFLIGHT, the POST-TEST phase automatically collects available evidence: goal/subtask completion ratios, artifact productivity (findings vs dead-ends), Sentinel gate decisions, and when available, test results and git metrics. Each evidence source has a quality weight (objective = 1.0, semi-objective = 0.7, inferred = 0.4) that modulates its influence on the grounded estimate.

With 7 grounded verifications across 25 evidence observations, results are preliminary but directionally informative:

Two patterns emerge even from this small sample:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{DO shows the largest overestimation} (+0.385). The AI assesses high execution capability, but objective evidence (goal completion ratios, artifact counts) indicates less was accomplished than claimed. This contrasts with the self-referential track, where DO underestimation dominates---the AI \emph{starts} conservative but \emph{finishes} overconfident.

    \item \textbf{KNOW and CONTEXT are well-calibrated} against grounded evidence (gaps $<$ 0.05), suggesting that for knowledge-related vectors, POSTFLIGHT self-assessment tracks objective outcomes accurately.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Track Divergence}

The self-referential track reports COMPLETION correction of +0.82 (massive underestimation at PREFLIGHT). But the grounded track shows COMPLETION overestimation of +0.125 at POSTFLIGHT. This divergence reveals a calibration arc: the AI starts too pessimistic, then overcorrects to too optimistic. The grounded track catches the overcorrection that the self-referential track cannot see.

Mean grounded coverage across verifications is 0.27 (27\% of vectors grounded per session), constrained by evidence availability. Sessions with richer evidence (goals, artifacts, and Sentinel data) achieve coverage up to 0.46.

\subsubsection{Implications}

Even with limited data, grounded verification shifts the epistemic landscape in two ways:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Limitation \#2 is partially addressed}: Self-assessment is no longer purely self-referential. For vectors with objective correlates, we can now measure calibration accuracy against outcomes.

    \item \textbf{Dual-track corrections are more trustworthy}: Where grounded evidence exists, the lower observation variance ($\sigma^2 = 0.05$ vs $0.10$) gives it stronger pull on the posterior, anchoring corrections in what happened rather than what the AI said happened.
\end{enumerate}

As evidence accumulates across domains---particularly in domains with richer objective signals like legal verification (\Cref{sec:discussion})---the grounded track will provide increasingly reliable calibration corrections.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations constrain interpretation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Observational design}: Data derives from naturally-occurring sessions, not controlled experiments with external ground truth.

    \item \textbf{Self-referential measurement}: POSTFLIGHT assessments are self-reported, and the learning delta measures self-consistency rather than accuracy against external ground truth. The grounded verification track (\Cref{tab:grounded-gaps}) partially addresses this by anchoring POSTFLIGHT assessments in objective evidence, but coverage remains limited (27\% of vectors per session) and the evidence base is small (N=7 verifications). Full external validation---human evaluation, automated test suites, downstream task performance---remains an open priority.

    \item \textbf{Model concentration}: 55\% of observations derive from Claude (Anthropic). Cross-model replication is needed.

    \item \textbf{Temporal confounding}: Data spans framework development. Patterns may reflect evolution rather than stable AI properties.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implications}

The unified confidence growth pattern---all 13 vectors moving toward higher confidence during learning---has several interpretations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Functional validity}: Self-assessment tracks actual epistemic state, with conservative priors corrected through evidence accumulation.

    \item \textbf{Training-induced conservatism}: RLHF and similar techniques may induce systematic underconfidence, which task execution naturally corrects.

    \item \textbf{Learning signature}: The pattern may simply reflect that doing work increases confidence---a tautology of task completion.
\end{enumerate}

The present contribution is empirical: \textbf{systematic confidence growth during learning is measurable and consistent}. The correction mechanism provides practical value regardless of causal interpretation.
